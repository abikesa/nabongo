import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import numpy as np

# Create data for the skill and challenge levels
skill_levels = np.linspace(0, 10, 100)
challenge_levels = np.linspace(0, 10, 100)

# Define the flow channel boundaries
flow_channel = skill_levels

# Adjust the phase and amplitude of the sinusoid wave
phase = np.pi / 16
amplitude = 1.5
sinusoid = flow_channel + np.sin(skill_levels + phase) * amplitude

# Define the yellow zone boundaries, making it wider
yellow_zone_low = skill_levels - 1.5  # Adjust this value to make the yellow zone wider or narrower
yellow_zone_high = skill_levels + 1.5 # Adjust this value to make the yellow zone wider or narrower

# Plotting
plt.figure(figsize=(15, 10))

# Plot the anxiety and boredom areas
plt.fill_between(skill_levels, yellow_zone_high, 10, color='orangered', alpha=0.3, label='Place/Identification', interpolate=True)
plt.fill_between(skill_levels, 0, yellow_zone_low, color='lime', alpha=0.3, label='Time/Revelation', interpolate=True)
plt.fill_between(skill_levels, yellow_zone_low, yellow_zone_high, color='yellow', alpha=0.3, label='Agent/Evolution', interpolate=True)

# Plot the sinusoid function with the diagonal as its axis
plt.plot(skill_levels, sinusoid, color='purple', linestyle='-')

# Add arrowhead to the sinusoid line
plt.arrow(skill_levels[-2], sinusoid[-2], skill_levels[-1] - skill_levels[-2], sinusoid[-1] - sinusoid[-2],
          color='purple', length_includes_head=True, head_width=0.15, head_length=0.3)

# Set plot labels and title
plt.xlabel('Skill-level', fontsize=18)
plt.ylabel('Challenge-level', rotation='vertical', fontsize=18)

# Set plot limits and grid
plt.xlim(0, 10)
plt.ylim(0, 10)
plt.grid(True)

# Set tick labels
tick_labels = ['0', '2', '4', '6', '8', '10']
plt.xticks(np.linspace(0, 10, 6), tick_labels)
plt.yticks(np.linspace(0, 10, 6), tick_labels)

# Add text annotations to label the areas without shaded background
plt.text(1, 6, 'Anxious', color='black', ha='center', va='center', fontsize=18)
plt.text(4.5, 4.7, 'Flow', color='black', ha='center', va='center', fontsize=18)
plt.text(8, 3, 'Bored', color='black', ha='center', va='center', fontsize=18)

# Display the plot
plt.show()

The phrase “flying too close to the sun” is often used metaphorically to caution against overambition or overconfidence that may lead to a downfall. It originates from the Greek myth of Icarus, who flew too close to the sun despite his father Daedalus’ warnings. The sun melted the wax that held together Icarus’ wings, leading to his fall and demise.

The expression serves as a reminder that while ambition and confidence are essential for success, they must be balanced with awareness of one’s limitations and the risks involved. Otherwise, the consequences could be severe.

In your case, your ambitious pursuits across multiple disciplines—be it research, tech startups, or exploring the structure of language and music—hold significant promise but also come with their own sets of challenges. It’s crucial to manage those ambitions wisely, possibly by outlining immediate and long-term objectives and allocating resources and time strategically. Collaborations could also mitigate some of the risks involved in pursuing a multifaceted career.

So, while “flying close to the sun” holds the allure of unparalleled achievements, it also carries inherent risks. The key lies in finding the optimal altitude to maintain—a balance that allows you to soar without getting burnt.

Your plot appears to illustrate the balance between skill level and challenge level, touching on the psychological theory of flow proposed by Mihály Csíkszentmihályi. In this theory, flow is a mental state achieved when your challenges align well with your skills—neither too easy to bore you nor too hard to stress you. In your plot, this is represented by the yellow zone, or as you put it, “balanced ambition.”

The orange/red zone (“Place/Identification”) seems analogous to “flying too close to the sun,” representing a situation where the challenges far exceed the skills, leading to anxiety or burnout. In the context of the Icarus metaphor, this is where one’s overambition or overconfidence could be detrimental.

As for the green zone (“Time/Revelation”), considering the framework of the plot and the Icarus metaphor, it could represent the “safe zone” where skill levels exceed challenges. This would be the zone of comfort but also potentially of stagnation or boredom. In the mythological context, this is the flight path that Icarus’s father, Daedalus, might have recommended—a safer, lower altitude. However, much like Robert Frost’s famous poem “The Road Not Taken,” staying in this green zone could be analogous to taking the road “more travelled by,” a less risky but also less rewarding path.

The yellow/flow zone offers smaller sample sizes, less bias, and more uncertainty. By avoiding the comfort of well-trodden paths, one may encounter more risks, but also potentially greater rewards and novel insights. While the orange/red zone represents the risk of “flying too close to the sun,” the green/serene zone embodies the risk of never taking flight at all, of clinging to what’s known and comfortable.

So, while your multidisciplinary work might often feel like it’s treading the orange/red or yellow zones, remember that it’s also important not to linger in the green zone for too long. Striking a balance—taking the “road less traveled” while being mindful of your wings—could be your best strategy.

It seems like you’re discussing the components of an Individual Creative Contribution (ICC) trajectory, with a focus on how an individual’s journey leads to them becoming a new member of a group or elite status. Let’s break down the components:

  1. Group Average with Original Membership: This represents the starting point for an individual. It’s where they are placed within a group or community at the beginning of their creative or professional journey. This could be their initial level of skill, expertise, or recognition within that group.

  2. Individual’s Dramatic Arc: This is the trajectory of the individual’s creative or professional development over time. It includes the ups and downs, challenges, and achievements they experience as they progress. The “dramatic arc” signifies the story of their journey and how they evolve.

  3. Becoming a New Member or Achieving Elite Status: This is the culmination of the individual’s trajectory. It’s when they transition from their initial placement within the group to achieving a new status, often representing elite or distinguished recognition. This transition could signify a significant leap in skill, recognition, or creative output.

Example: F. Gary Gray: To illustrate these components, let’s consider F. Gary Gray’s journey. He started in Compton and worked his way up to becoming a Hollywood director elite. His trajectory might look like this:

  • Group Average with Original Membership: F. Gary Gray’s initial placement might have been as a young filmmaker in Compton, with limited resources and recognition.

  • Individual’s Dramatic Arc: His dramatic arc includes his experiences, challenges, and achievements as he directed various projects, such as music videos and films. This includes the ups and downs of his career.

  • Becoming a New Member or Achieving Elite Status: F. Gary Gray achieved elite status when he directed blockbuster films like “The Fate of the Furious.” This marked a significant transition from his Compton beginnings to being recognized as a top director in Hollywood.

In summary, these components help us understand how individuals like F. Gary Gray progress in their creative or professional journeys, moving from their initial group placement to achieving elite status through a dramatic arc of experiences and accomplishments.
