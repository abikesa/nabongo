How fear has shaped human affairs

A new history argues that power depends on frightening people

image: anthony gerace

Sep 9th 2023

Listen to this story. Enjoy more audio and podcasts on iOS or Android.

Fear: An Alternative History of the World. By Robert Peckham. Profile Books; 448 pages; ¬£25

One of the bestselling books of all time was about witches. It was not a cheerful romp like the Harry Potter novels. Instead, it was a serious instruction manual, explaining how to identify, capture, torture confessions from‚Äîand eventually kill‚ÄîSatan‚Äôs handmaidens. Knowing nothing of microbes, people guessed that misfortunes such as disease were caused by magic. Yet their fear could be manipulated for political purposes. Exterminating witches was a handy way for an earthly ruler to signal which side he was on: God‚Äôs, not the Devil‚Äôs.

Fear is a primal, necessary emotion. Mr Peckham calls it ‚Äúa neurobiological process to keep us alive‚Äù. If our ancestors had not feared cliff-edges or sabre-toothed tigers, we might not be here today. The flipside is that, since humans are a communicative, imaginative species, fear can be conjured out of whispers. His book does not quite live up to its ambitious subtitle, but it illuminates the many ways in which fear has shaped human behaviour over the past 700 years, from which readers can draw lessons for the present.

The main one is: ‚ÄúPower depends on fear.‚Äù Mr Peckham argues that the turmoil of the Reformation and Counter-Reformation stemmed partly from the Catholic church‚Äôs loss of its ‚Äúmonopoly on fear‚Äù in western Europe. (Conspiracy theories about Jews persist today, many of them directed at the Rothschilds)

Covid was less awful than the Black Death. Medicine has advanced over the centuries, but human nature has changed rather less. Fear of the coronavirus seeded all manner of conspiracy theories, and many governments took advantage of pandemic panic to suppress civil liberties. While living in Hong Kong, Mr Peckham observed first-hand how the Chinese Communist Party used the virus as an excuse to ban the pro-democracy protests that had rocked the place in 2019-20.

But the fatwa had global consequences, making clear that the anti-blasphemy laws of a reactionary theocracy could be enforced by volunteer assassins anywhere on the planet.
